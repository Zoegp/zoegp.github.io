How to use LeafByte

LeafByte is best used with images like you see here: a leaf on a solid background without significant shadow. The background is much lighter than the leaf to clearly differentiate the two (LeafByte can also be used with light tissue on dark backgrounds). The leaf is surrounded by four dots, which constitute the scale. The scale's four dots make a square of known size, which gives a frame of reference both for determining absolute areas in an image and for correcting the image if it was taken at an angle (LeafByte can also be used without the scale, but you won't get absolute measurements or angle correction). The size of the scale is measured as the distance between two dots on the same side of the square, from dot center to dot center. You can download a document with scales of different sizes [here]; after printing a page, just measure exactly how large the scale ended up given your specific printer. LeafByte also works fine if you make your own scale marking, e.g. spray paint on a tarp for very large leaves. For best results, don't use a scale that's excessively larger than your samples, or else the samples will take up a very small portion of your images and have poor resolution.

You'll see the title screen upon first opening LeafByte. From here, the standard way to proceed is with one of the two large buttons near the bottom, choosing whether you want to use an image that already exists or take a new photo. Alternatively, you can configure various settings for LeafByte [link to later] in the top right or begin a tutorial in the top left.

After choosing an image or taking a photo, you'll begin with background removal. The background must be removed suffiently to separate the leaf and the scale marking
